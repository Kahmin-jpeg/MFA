01

Framing the Inquiry

Untangling Access in Wheelchair Service Delivery

Nobody wants to need a wheelchair. But when you do, how long should you wait to move, to leave your home, to live your life?

The 100-Day Reality

On average, it takes over 100 days to receive a mobility device in the U.S. (Cuppett et al., 2022). The wheelchair service delivery system is highly fragmented and bureaucratic, involving multiple steps, clinic appointments, evaluations, supplier documentation, and insurer authorization‚Äîeach prone to delays, miscommunication, and unclear accountability (McDoom et al., 2012).

Stakeholders often operate in silos with limited system-wide visibility. Regulatory frameworks, particularly those tied to Medicare/Medicaid, contribute to prolonged disruptions, while clinicians face heavy documentation burdens (Hobensack, 2022), suppliers are constrained by repair policies, and users have little clarity or control over their options (Smith, 2024).

Fragmented Process Policy Constraints Stakeholder Silos Documentation Burden

Research Context

What do people know?

While there has been substantial quantitative research involving multiple stakeholders, and qualitative studies focused on individual groups, there remains a notable gap in research that qualitatively examines the wheelchair service delivery process through the active participation of all key parties‚Äîwheelchair users, caregivers, clinicians, manufacturers, and insurers‚Äîusing a co-design approach.

Design-Led Systems Approach

This thesis adopts a design-led, systems-oriented approach grounded in co-design to explore the complex and often fragmented landscape of wheelchair service delivery in the United States. Rather than attempting to solve a predefined problem, the research operates within the "fuzzy front end" of the design process‚Äîwhere ambiguity, misalignment, and systemic opacity obscure opportunities for meaningful change.

Research Methodology

This research adopts co-design as the primary research approach, leveraging participatory tools and workshop formats to explore systemic barriers and reimagine the wheelchair service delivery model. Rooted in design-led inquiry, the approach aims to surface lived experiences and collective visions for change through a series of structured, yet open-ended, engagements with diverse stakeholders.

Why Co-Design is Necessary?

1

Complexity Is Social, Not Just Technical

The wheelchair service delivery process is a network of human relationships shaped by policy, expectations, and lived experience. Co-design makes these relational dynamics visible and negotiable.

2

Lived Experience Is Missing from Policy

Regulatory frameworks are top-down and disconnected from user experience. Co-design centers lived experience as legitimate expertise, allowing direct stakeholder input.

3

Stakeholders Operate in Silos

The process is siloed, with actors seeing only a slice of the system. Co-design workshops enable dialogue, shared sense-making, and co-creation of solutions.

4

Policy Change Requires Systemic Buy-In

Traditional reforms fail without real-world grounding. Co-design builds trust, shared language, and mutual accountability for adaptive, implementable shifts.

Pilot Tests & Platform Choice

The primary research was initiated with a series of workshop materials development for the co-design sessions. Following ethical guidelines, several questions arose about the effectiveness and clarity of materials.

Key refinements for pilot testing:

  • ‚úì Are the materials used helpful?
  • ‚úì What are the areas of improvement that can be made?
  • ‚úì How can I better prepare/know what to anticipate for the actual workshop?

Platform Choice: Miro

Given the sensitive nature of the topic, hosting the co-design workshop in person at Hayes Hall at The Ohio State University proved to be less practical. With participants joining from across the Midwest, conducting the workshop on Miro offered a more accessible and meaningful platform. It provided greater flexibility, ensured participant comfort, and ultimately added more value to the co-design process.

Co-Design Activities

Pre-Workshop Preparation

Before each session, participants received preparatory materials visualizing the current Rehabilitation Engineering and Assistive Technology Society of North America (RESNA) wheelchair service delivery framework from a clinician's perspective. These materials provided a shared reference point and foundational context for subsequent discussions.

Individual Experience Mapping

During the workshop, participants engaged in a visual mapping exercise to reflect on their personal journey within the wheelchair service delivery system. Using a temporal axis, individuals identified positive experiences above the line and challenges below the line. This activity facilitated self-guided storytelling while anchoring narratives in specific points of interaction.

Future Visioning and Speculative Scenarios

Participants transitioned from present experience to speculation. Working individually or in small groups, they co-created speculative scenarios, either utopian or dystopian, to reimagine the future of wheelchair service delivery. A creative canvas (Miro board) supported the construction of visual narratives.

Surfacing Insights from the System

Analytical Approach: DIKW Framework and Coding Methods

Analysis Approach

The analysis focused on making sense of data gathered through co-design workshops, stakeholder interviews, and visual mapping exercises that examined the complexities of the wheelchair service delivery process. Emphasis was placed on capturing the lived experiences.

Transcript Review

Audio-recorded co-design sessions and interviews were transcribed and reviewed alongside participant-generated artifacts such as annotated maps, visual timelines, and storytelling prompts. Particular attention was paid to emotionally charged language, tensions in service interactions, and moments where participants described agency, resistance, or systemic breakdowns.

Affinity Mapping

Using Miro, I clustered key quotes, reflections, and observational notes across stakeholder types. This allowed for the identification of both overlapping and diverging themes, such as breakdown points in authorization, repair delays, or emotional labor within caregiver roles. Color-coded tags were used to distinguish between systemic, relational, and procedural insights.

Thematic Coding and Synthesis

Insights were synthesized through iterative coding rounds to highlight recurring patterns in participant narratives. Codes were organized around phases of the wheelchair acquisition journey (e.g., prescription, funding approval, delivery, maintenance), as well as cross-cutting themes such as autonomy, opacity, and trust.

Key Insights

Insights from the co-design sessions revealed distinct yet interconnected themes across stakeholder groups, highlighting critical friction points and relational breakdowns within the wheelchair service delivery process. Each group brought forward unique lived experiences that collectively demonstrate how systemic inefficiencies ripple across the broader ecosystem.

Finding:

Taken together, these findings reveal a deeply entangled network of relationships, responsibilities, and constraints that shape the wheelchair service delivery process. While each stakeholder group faces distinct challenges, the data points to a shared experience of fragmentation, miscommunication, and misalignment between policy intent and practice.

Visualization and Evaluation

Development of the Interactive Visual System Model

Development of the Interactive Visual System Model

Given the widespread fragmentation and misalignment surfaced across stakeholder narratives, it becomes clear that understanding the wheelchair service delivery system requires more than identifying individual breakdowns; it demands a collective reframing of how relationships, policies, and processes interact over time.

An initial visual system mapping intervention was developed to address these relational gaps. The intervention aimed not only to make complexity visible but also to support participatory reflection across stakeholder groups. This composite model was visualized using a two-dimensional journey framework: the vertical axis represented stakeholder roles, while the horizontal axis represented time.

How was this assembled?

To synthesize findings across stakeholder experiences, we developed a visual system mapping tool that emerged from participant-created experience maps and lived experiences through the co-design sessions. This intervention consisted the results of analyzed diagrams and annotated maps built directly from the participants' workshop inputs.

Based on user feedback, the visualization was reconceptualized to:

Provide each stakeholder group a lens into the full system, not just their silo

Reveal points of tension between policy and practice

Highlight invisible labor performed by users and caregivers

Show how regulatory feedback loops delay or derail progress

Mapping Stakeholder Dynamics and Invisible Labor

Ultimately, the intervention produced through this research functions as a set of tools that support engagement with system complexity. Rather than acting as explanatory illustrations, these figures function as tools for cross-stakeholder dialogue, enabling participants to see their role in a broader system and identify opportunities for redesign.

Reflections and Systemic Implications

Final Visualizations, Policy Design, and Future Research

Final Visualizations

This research demonstrates how systemic design, grounded in relational perspectives and participatory methods, can help untangle the complexities of wheelchair service delivery. By surfacing the hidden dynamics of stakeholder relationships and policy constraints, we move toward more equitable, adaptive, and transparent models of healthcare access.

Our visual tools and co-design approach offer practical starting points for reimagining policy governance as a living, iterative process, where impact is not a singular outcome but an evolving pattern of relational change.

Implications for Policy Design, Collaboration, and Equity

Several practical implications emerged from this research:

Policy Participation

Policies can no longer be designed in a vacuum. Incorporating qualitative user narratives into insurance frameworks could make eligibility determinations more transparent and equitable.

Cross-Stakeholder Dialogue

Involved stakeholders must be allowed and supported to collaborate meaningfully. Restrictive documentation practices currently hinder this relationship.

Visual Tools as System Navigators

The interactive visual model created during this project served not only as research tools but also as system navigation aids, empowering participants.

Limitations & Future Works

While this research focused primarily on the wheelchair service delivery model in the United States, specifically Ohio, future research could expand this framework to other health systems and geographies. Where future-focused scenarios were imaginative but not stress-tested against legal or financial feasibility.

Additional work is needed to explore how these insights can be operationalized within policymaking and education, particularly within spaces that address systemic and social design challenges. As future research moves toward implementation, attention must be paid to how relational, participatory approaches can coexist with the structures and logics of hierarchical systems.

Key Consideration

Budget-controlling departments are unlikely to adopt co-design approaches unless their value is translated into system-compatible formats such as improved outcomes, faster processing times, or reduced administrative burden. Exploring this translation will be crucial to scaling this work beyond the prototyping phase.
